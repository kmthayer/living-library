The Thayer Lab Living Library

The Thayer Lab Living Library is an ongoing and expanding archival project. Holdings continue to be inventoried and added to the searchable catalog.

The Thayer Lab Living Library is a scholarly collection centered on computational molecular biophysics, physical chemistry, molecular simulation, scientific computing, and related areas of interdisciplinary science. Computational molecular biophysics is itself an inherently interdisciplinary field, drawing together chemistry, physics, biology, mathematics, computer science, and increasingly artificial intelligence. As a result, the collection reflects not only the professional and intellectual interests of its curator, but also the broader interdisciplinary nature of modern scientific inquiry.

The library also contains selected works in theology, literature, mathematics, history, music, pedagogy, philosophy, and the history of science. Together, these holdings reflect both scholarly specialization and the broader habit of reading across disciplines for academic development, intellectual curiosity, and personal fulfillment.

The collection originates from the personal library of Kelly M. Thayer. Over the past four decades, books have been gathered through formal study, research, teaching, personal interest, conference participation, donations, library sales, free tables, and institutional transitions. The collection traces an academic journey through institutions including Foxborough High School, Regis College, Wesleyan University, Northwestern University, the University of Massachusetts Medical School, the Massachusetts Institute of Technology, Assumption College, and Vassar College.

The core holdings were formed through books acquired during high school, undergraduate, graduate, and postdoctoral studies. Over time, the library expanded through research acquisitions, teaching materials, conference volumes, donated works, used book finds, and materials rescued from library discards and building relocations across multiple institutions. A substantial portion of the collection derives from the library of David L. Beveridge, whose contributions significantly expanded the holdings in molecular biophysics, statistical mechanics, and molecular simulation. Additional contributions were received from the collections of Michael P. Weir and David R. Langley.

The collection reflects both an interdisciplinary scientific identity and the process through which that identity was formed. Reading broadly across disciplines, both for academic purposes and personal fulfillment, shaped the intellectual development of the collection and its curator alike.

The Living Library is so named for several reasons. The contents of the collection largely trace the physical and intellectual trajectory of a life in science, teaching, and scholarship. Many holdings reflect ideas being explored at particular moments in time, especially the original notebooks, papers, folders, and working collections assembled throughout years of study and research. This approach to gathering and organizing information owes much to the example and advice of mentors, especially the encouragement to “make yourself a notebook.”

The library is also “living” because it is never truly finished. New books continue to enter the collection because they represent new ideas, new questions, and new connections. While digital libraries and electronic readers increasingly define the future of scholarship, physical books remain uniquely meaningful objects. Many of these volumes have traveled in backpacks, accumulated notes in margins, absorbed the evidence of late-night study and working meals, and persisted as companions through multiple stages of life and career.

Some books in the collection have developed lives of their own through repeated use by students and colleagues. After lending a particular copy of Stryer Biochemistry to numerous students over the years, a small tradition emerged in which borrowers began leaving thank-you notes attached to the book. The next student noticed the growing collection of notes and added another. Over time, the worn volume, with its cracked binding, tattered corners, annotations, and accumulated messages, became less a solitary object and more an ongoing conversation across time among the students and colleagues who learned from it.

The Living Library is also, quite literally, a live-in library. The books occupy offices, laboratories, bedrooms, hallways, shelves, closets, storage spaces, and the homes of friends, students, and colleagues to whom they are lent. They are intended to be used. Learning is inseparable from research and teaching, and books remain one of the primary mechanisms by which ideas persist across generations. In this sense, the collection represents not merely an archive of information, but an ongoing ecosystem of intellectual life.

Members of the Wesleyan University community interested in borrowing materials are welcome to contact Prof. Kelly M. Thayer or visit during office hours to arrange access.
